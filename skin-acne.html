Skin Concern · Acne

Acne-Prone Skin.

A clear, evidence-based guide to understanding and treating acne-prone skin — without the myths.

How to know if this is you

LUMEVA
01
Recurring breakouts in the same zones.

Acne tends to cluster on the forehead, chin, and jawline (hormonal) or cheeks (bacterial/friction). Recurring breakouts in the same spot points to a consistent trigger rather than random bad luck.

LUMEVA
02
Blackheads and whiteheads.

These are comedones — pores clogged with oil and dead skin. Blackheads are open and oxidised (dark), whiteheads are closed. Both are the earliest, mildest stage of acne.

LUMEVA
03
Inflamed, red, or tender bumps.

Papules and pustules form when clogged pores become inflamed by bacteria (C. acnes). Tenderness to touch signals active inflammation, not just clogged pores.

LUMEVA
04
Oily T-zone by midday.

Excess sebum production is the primary driver of acne. If your forehead and nose look shiny by noon despite a morning cleanse, oil production is elevated.

LUMEVA
05
Post-inflammatory marks after healing.

Dark or red marks that linger after a pimple clears (PIH/PIE) are common in acne-prone skin and require separate treatment from the active breakout itself.

LUMEVA
06
Breakouts tied to your cycle or stress.

Hormonal fluctuations increase sebum production. If breakouts spike predictably around your period or high-stress periods, hormonal acne is likely a factor.

Root causes

Understanding the cause helps you target the right solution rather than guessing.

🦠

C. acnes bacteria

A normal skin bacterium that overgrows in clogged, oxygen-poor pores, triggering inflammation and pustules.

💧

Excess sebum production

Androgens (hormones) stimulate oil glands to overproduce sebum, which mixes with dead skin to clog pores.

🧬

Genetics

Family history is one of the strongest predictors of acne severity and duration — if your parents had acne, you are statistically more likely to.

📱

Friction and occlusion

Phone screens, masks, helmets, and tight collars trap heat and bacteria against skin, causing "acne mechanica" along contact points.

🍬

High-glycemic diet

Diets high in refined sugar and dairy have been linked in clinical studies to increased acne severity via insulin and IGF-1 pathways.

😰

Stress and cortisol

Cortisol increases sebum production and slows healing, which is why breakouts often appear during exam periods, deadlines, or emotional stress.

Dermatologist note

Acne is a medical condition with genetic, hormonal, and bacterial components — it is never caused by poor hygiene alone, and harsh scrubbing makes it worse. Mild to moderate acne typically responds to consistent OTC treatment (salicylic acid, benzoyl peroxide, niacinamide) within 8-12 weeks. If you have painful, deep cystic acne, acne leaving significant scarring, or no improvement after 3 months of consistent treatment, please see a dermatologist — prescription options (topical retinoids, oral antibiotics, hormonal treatment) are highly effective and accessible.
